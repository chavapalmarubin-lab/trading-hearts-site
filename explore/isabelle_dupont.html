Fastrada Coin of Charlemagne

Centre Charlemagne · 2022 acquisition · 793-794 AD
A silver denier bearing the name of Fastrada, Charlemagne's third wife, acquired by the Centre Charlemagne in 2022.
A coin minted for a queen who died within the year — dated so precisely by her death that we know almost the exact week Isabelle's story begins.

Charlemagne Portrait Denarius (Temple Type)

Centre Charlemagne · Temple type denier · c. 813-814 AD
An extremely rare silver coin showing Charlemagne in classical portrait style, only about 50 known to survive.
Charlemagne wanted to look like a Roman emperor on his own money — proof that even the most powerful man in Europe still needed a Roman stamp of legitimacy.

Carolingian Denier (Mainz Mint)

Bibliothèque nationale de France · catalogue ark:/12148/cb386395898 · 812-814 AD
A unique surviving silver denier from the Mainz mint, part of the Bibliothèque nationale de France's medieval coin collection.
One of a kind — literally the only one known to survive from its mint and date, a single silver witness to an entire monetary system.

Aachen Penny of Charlemagne

Referenced via academic literature · 2008 archaeological find · c. 800-814 AD
A Carolingian penny found through archaeological excavation in 2008, part of the standardized reform coinage.
Dug out of the ground centuries later, still legible — Charlemagne's reform was built to last, and this coin is the proof.

KAROLUS Monogram Denier

Various — referenced via Encyclopedia Britannica · Monogram type · c. 793-814 AD
A silver denier bearing Charlemagne's monogram, struck across France, Germany, Northern Italy, and Northeast Spain.
The same stamped symbol, accepted from Barcelona to the Rhine — this is the coin that first made 'French money' mean something across borders.
