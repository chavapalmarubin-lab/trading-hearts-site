Athenian Owl Tetradrachm

British Museum · 1905,0309.2 · c. 449-413 BCE
A silver four-drachma coin from Athens stamped with the goddess Athena on one side and her sacred owl on the other.
This is the coin that taught the ancient world to trust a stranger's stamp instead of weighing metal by hand — the moment money stopped being a substance and started being a promise.

Athenian Dekadrachm

British Museum · G.3183 · c. 467-465 BCE
An extremely rare ten-drachma silver coin, one of the largest denominations Athens ever struck.
Big coins for big moments — this one was minted when Athens wanted the world to see its silver mines were the richest on earth.

Lydian Electrum Stater

British Museum · 1866,1201.3669 · c. 600 BCE
One of the earliest coins ever struck, made from a natural gold-silver alloy in the kingdom of Lydia.
Before Alexandros had a face or a name, this rough lump of stamped metal was the very first draft of the idea of money.

Where this money travelled

Alexandros' silver drachma began in the mines south of Athens and traveled along trade routes reaching from Egypt to Central Asia, becoming the first truly international currency.
