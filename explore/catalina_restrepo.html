Muisca Raft (Balsa Muisca)

Museo del Oro (Gold Museum), Banco de la República · Not publicly listed · c. 1295–1410 AD
A miniature raft bearing a central gold-dusted chief (zipa) surrounded by attendant figures with ceremonial staffs and headdresses. Depicts the El Dorado gilding ceremony at Lake Guatavita.
The single most iconic artifact of Muisca goldwork. The real object behind the El Dorado legend — a Colombian national emblem that predates the peso by 400 years.

Gold 10 Peso Coin — Cóndor Design (Popayán Mint)

American Numismatic Society (ANS) · 1960.166.309 · 1853
Obverse: Liberty bust with 'REPUBLICA DE LA NUEVA GRANADA · LIBERTAD'. Reverse: Andean condor with 'LIBERTAD Y ORDEN'. Established by the Law of 30 May 1853 as the gold cóndor standard at 10 pesos.
The coin that fixed Colombia's gold standard and gave the condor — its national bird — a permanent place on its money. The gold anchor of the peso for three decades.

Casa de Moneda de Bogotá

Museo Casa de Moneda (Banco de la República) · N/A (building) · Founded 1620–1621; milled coinage from 1756
The first mint of the Nuevo Reino de Granada, in La Candelaria neighborhood. Colonial cobs, Republic-era pesos, and gold cóndores were all struck here over three centuries.
The physical origin point of all Colombian coinage — from hand-hammered colonial gold to the modern peso. Still standing and operating as a museum.

Colombia 8 Reales Silver Coin, 1821

Wikimedia Commons (digitized from collector source) · N/A · 1821
A milled silver 8-reales coin struck at the transition between Spanish colonial rule and independence — part of the 'piece of eight' tradition dating to 1627. The monetary bridge between colonial Real and Republic peso.
The coin Catalina was born from — the peso's direct ancestor. The 8 reales was the world's first global currency; the peso replaced it.

Comisión Corográfica Watercolor — 'View of the Mountains above Cali'

Library of Congress (digital); Biblioteca Nacional de Colombia (physical archive) · LOC item 2021670051 · c. 1853–1859
Field illustration by artist Manuel María Paz, created during the government-commissioned Comisión Corográfica survey of New Granada led by Agustín Codazzi. Documents landscapes, costumes, and daily life.

Where this money travelled

Born in the heart of the Andes, Colombia's peso traces a journey from Muisca gold ceremonies at Lake Guatavita through colonial mints in Bogotá and Popayán, along Caribbean trade routes from Cartagena, and outward to international museum collections that hold the surviving evidence of her story.
