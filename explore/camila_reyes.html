Chop-Marked 8 Reales Coin

Smithsonian National Museum of American History · NU.68.159.2418 · 1805
A Spanish colonial silver 8-reales coin stamped repeatedly with Chinese merchant chop marks, proof it circulated across Asia.
Every chop mark on this coin is a stranger, somewhere on the other side of the world, deciding they trusted Camila's silver enough to stamp their own name on it too.

Codex Mendoza

Bodleian Library, University of Oxford · MS. Arch. Selden. A. 1 · c. 1541
An Aztec pictorial and Spanish-annotated manuscript detailing tribute, economy, and daily life in early colonial Mexico.
Before the peso, there was this — a record of an entire economy of tribute and trade that the Spanish silver dollar would eventually replace and erase.

Florentine Codex

Getty Research Institute (digital edition) · Florentine Codex digital edition · 1575-1577
A monumental illustrated ethnographic work documenting Aztec culture, compiled under Spanish friar Bernardino de Sahagún.
This is the last full portrait of a world before Camila's silver economy swallowed it whole.

Casa Nacional de Moneda de Potosí

UNESCO World Heritage Site / Casa Nacional de la Moneda · UNESCO Site ref 420 · founded 1572
The colonial mint building in Potosí, Bolivia, where much of the silver that became Spanish colonial pesos was struck.
This building turned mountain silver into global money — and worked its laborers to death doing it. Camila carries that weight in every coin she represents.

Macuquina (Cob Coinage)

Banco de México numismatic collection · Referenced in Banco de México archive · 16th-18th century
Roughly hand-cut irregular silver coins produced in colonial Spanish American mints, predecessors to the milled 8-reales.
Crude, uneven, hand-cut — but every irregular edge on these coins carried real silver value across oceans and empires.
