Coenwulf Gold Mancus

British Museum · 2006,0204.1 · c. 805-810 AD
An extraordinarily rare Anglo-Saxon gold coin from the successor system to Offa's silver penny standard.
Only a handful of these survive — a reminder that behind every steady currency is a moment so fragile that almost all the evidence of it was lost to time.

Cynethryth Penny (Offa's Queen)

British Museum · CM TYS / BMC 60 · c. 787-792 AD
A silver penny struck in the name of Cynethryth, wife of King Offa — the only Anglo-Saxon queen ever to have coinage struck in her own name.
A woman's name on money, thirteen centuries before anyone thought that was normal. Charlotte's whole story starts with someone insisting she deserved to be named.

Silver Penny of Offa (East Anglian mint)

Fitzwilliam Museum · media-208429 · c. 780-793 AD
A silver penny of King Offa of Mercia, struck by the moneyer Lul, establishing the 240-pennies-to-a-pound system.
This is the coin that invented the pound as a unit — the ancestor of every British pound that would ever be spent.

Sutton Hoo Helmet

British Museum · 1939,1010.93 · early 7th century AD
The iconic ceremonial helmet from the Sutton Hoo ship burial, the richest Anglo-Saxon grave ever found.
Charlotte's world was already this rich and ceremonial before a single coin bore her likeness — this helmet is what wealth looked like before money had a face.

Staffordshire Hoard

Birmingham Museums Trust · Staffordshire Hoard collection · c. 650-675 AD
The largest hoard of Anglo-Saxon gold and silver metalwork ever found, unearthed in a Staffordshire field in 2009.
Buried in a hurry and never reclaimed — a whole kingdom's wealth left in the dirt, waiting 1,300 years to tell its story.
